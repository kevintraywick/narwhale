Thesis
Clayton Christensen's framework combines two lenses. "Jobs to Be Done" asks: what job is the customer hiring this product to do, and does it serve the functional, emotional, and social dimensions of that job? The "Four-Box Business Model" tests whether the business has the four interlocking elements needed to deliver on the promise: a compelling customer value proposition, a viable profit formula, the key resources to execute, and the key processes to deliver repeatably. The disruption type (low-end, new-market, or head-on) signals defensibility — new-market disruption is the strongest position because incumbents have no incentive to respond.

New-Market Disruption
Jobs to Be Done
JTBD ClarityStrong
The job is crystal clear: "I need a place to stay that I can afford, and I want it to feel like more than a sterile hotel room." For hosts: "I have spare space and want to make money from it." Both jobs are well-articulated and the product directly addresses them. The DNC launch proved that when hotels sell out, the job becomes urgent.
Functional DimensionStrong
Save money (travelers pay less than hotels), make money (hosts earn from spare space). The functional value is immediate, quantifiable, and demonstrated — $75-100/night listings versus $200+ hotel rates during events. The product functionally works: search, browse, book, pay.
Emotional + Social DimensionsStrong
"Share culture — local connection to the city." The testimonials show this isn't marketing copy — Dan from Ontario says he values "the ideas, the interactions, the people" and "you don't get that in a hotel room." The emotional dimension (feeling welcomed, authentic experience) and social dimension (cultural exchange, personal connection) are genuine differentiators that hotels structurally cannot replicate.
Four-Box Business Model
Customer Value PropositionStrong
Clearly differentiated from every alternative: cheaper than hotels, safer than Craigslist, monetizable unlike Couchsurfing, more personal than VRBO. The competition slide maps this positioning visually — AB&B sits in the "affordable + online transaction" quadrant that nobody else occupies. The CVP serves both sides of the marketplace with clear, distinct benefits.
Profit FormulaAdequate
10% commission on each transaction is clear and marketplace-standard. The math: 84M trips × $25 avg fee = $2.1B at scale. For year 1: 80K transactions × $25 = $2M. The model works in theory, but at $25 per transaction the margins are thin after payment processing (2-3%), customer support, and fraud costs. No discussion of how margins improve at scale or what the cost structure looks like.
Key ResourcesAdequate
The founding team covers design and engineering. The live product is a key resource. The Craigslist integration hack provides a supply acquisition channel. Michael Seibel brings startup network. But the team lacks marketplace operations expertise, and there's no trust/safety infrastructure — which will become the single most important resource as the platform scales.
Key ProcessesWeak
This is the weakest element. No discussion of: identity verification (how do you know the host is real?), payment escrow (what happens if the listing is fake?), damage protection (what if a guest trashes the place?), dispute resolution (who arbitrates conflicts?), or quality assurance (how do you maintain listing accuracy?). These processes are existential for a trust-dependent marketplace and they're entirely absent from the deck.
5 / 7 sub-elements strong or adequate
