Idea Quality
PG1Organic Problem DiscoveryPass
"How to Get Startup Ideas"
The founders couldn't afford a hotel for a design conference in San Francisco. They put air mattresses on their floor and charged guests. The idea came from living the problem, not brainstorming it.
PG2Schlep BlindnessPass
"Schlep Blindness"
"Let strangers sleep in your home" is deeply uncomfortable for most people and investors. The trust, safety, insurance, and logistics problems are massive schleps that most founders would avoid. That's the opportunity.
PG3Frighteningly AmbitiousPass
"Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas"
The market size slide says it all: 2 billion+ trips booked worldwide, $2.1B projected revenue. They're proposing to reshape how humans find accommodation when they travel. That's scary-big.
PG4Do Things That Don't ScalePass
"Do Things That Don't Scale"
Targeted the DNC specifically (one event, one city). Craigslist dual-posting is a manual hack. Events strategy (Octoberfest, Cebit, Mardi Gras) means city-by-city, event-by-event hustle. Classic unscalable early tactics.
PG5The Narrow WedgePass
"How to Get Startup Ideas"
Started with a razor-sharp wedge: budget travelers attending events where hotels sell out. Not "anyone traveling anywhere" — specifically events (DNC, Cebit, Octoberfest) where supply/demand imbalance is extreme.
PG6Made Something People WantPass
"Be Good"
User testimonials with real names and cities. Press coverage from 4 outlets. Real bookings at real prices. People are using it and saying things like "freaking rocks" and "a complete success."
Founder Quality
PG7Determination Over IntelligencePass
"The Anatomy of Determination"
They famously sold Obama O's and Cap'n McCains cereal boxes to fund the company when no one would invest. They've been rejected by every VC on Sand Hill Road. They're still here, still building.
PG8Cofounder DepthPass
"The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups"
Three cofounders with genuinely complementary skills: Joe (UI/UX + PR), Brian (business development + brand), Nathan (engineering). Joe and Brian are RISD classmates with a real working relationship. Nathan brings Harvard CS + Microsoft.
PG9Technical FoundersPass
"The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups"
Nathan Blecharcyzk is a Harvard CS graduate who worked at Microsoft and OPNET, and built Facebook apps with 75,000 users. The product is built and live — the technical cofounder delivered.
PG10ResourcefulnessPass
"Relentlessly Resourceful"
The Craigslist cross-posting hack is peak resourcefulness — piggybacking on Craigslist's 50K weekly housing listings to bootstrap supply. Targeting sold-out events for instant demand. Cereal boxes to fund operations. This team finds a way.
PG11Flexibility on PathPass
"How Not to Die"
Already pivoted from "air beds during conferences" to "book rooms with locals." The name still says AirBed but the product has evolved to full rooms and apartments. Shows willingness to follow where users lead.
PG12Domain ObsessionFail
"How to Do Great Work"
The founders are designers who stumbled into travel. There's no evidence of deep hospitality industry knowledge or years of travel industry experience. The passion is for the product and the design, not the domain itself.
Execution & Growth
PG13Growth RateFail
"Startup = Growth"
No growth metrics in the deck. No week-over-week bookings, no monthly user growth, no trajectory chart. The target is 80K transactions in 12 months but no current run rate is shown.
PG14Launch SpeedPass
"The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups"
Already launched. Live website, real listings across 108 cities in 17 countries, real bookings, real testimonials. Not a pitch about what they're going to build — it's live.
PG15Default Alive or Default DeadFail
"Default Alive or Default Dead"
Default dead. At the current stage, revenue from 10% commissions on a small number of bookings can't sustain three founders in San Francisco. They need the $500K to survive.
PG16Ramen ProfitableFail
"Ramen Profitability"
Not ramen profitable — the cereal box stunt was literally to fund operations. Revenue from bookings at this stage is negligible compared to San Francisco living costs for three people.
PG17High-Touch Early SalesPass
"Do Things That Don't Scale"
Events strategy = manually targeting specific conferences city by city. Craigslist integration = hand-to-hand supply acquisition. They later went door-to-door photographing listings. This is textbook high-touch.
Culture & Strategy
PG18Don't Play HousePass
"The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups"
Three guys working from their apartment (literally the one they rented out). No fancy office, no advisory board theater, no conference circuit. Selling cereal to stay alive. Pure hustle.
PG19Benevolent to UsersPass
"Be Good"
Genuinely serves both sides: travelers save money and get authentic experiences; hosts make money from spare space. The "share culture" value proposition is sincere — user testimonials confirm people value the human connection.
PG20Learned From PredecessorsPass
"Before the Startup"
The competition slide is one of the strongest in the deck — maps Couchsurfing (free, no transactions), Craigslist (offline, no trust), VRBO (vacation rentals, expensive), Hotels.com (expensive), and positions AB&B in the affordable + online transaction sweet spot. They know exactly where everyone else sits and why there's a gap.
